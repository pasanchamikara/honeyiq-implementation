\chapter{Results}
\label{ch:results}

\section{Overview}

This chapter reports quantitative outcomes from three sets of experiments:
(1) DQN training on the \emph{Opportunistic} attacker intent,
(2) Random Forest classifier evaluation, and
(3) cross-intent policy transfer.
All results were obtained using the hyperparameters listed in
Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}.

\section{Training Curves}

\subsection{Episode Reward}

Figure~\ref{fig:training_curves} (top-left panel) shows episode reward over
500 training episodes.
Early episodes exhibit high variance as the policy explores randomly
($\varepsilon \approx 1$).
A clear upward trend emerges after approximately 100 episodes, consistent
with the replay buffer accumulating diverse transitions.
The rolling 10-episode mean stabilises in the final third of training,
indicating policy convergence.

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figures/training_curves}
  \caption{Training curves: episode reward (top-left), detection rate
           (top-centre), false-positive rate (top-right), average threat
           level (bottom-left), per-update DQN loss (bottom-centre), and
           per-episode average DQN loss (bottom-right).}
  \label{fig:training_curves}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Detection Rate}

The detection rate (TP\,/\,(TP+FN)) climbs from approximately 0.3--0.5 in
early episodes to above 0.8 by the end of training.
The improvement correlates temporally with the decline in $\varepsilon$,
confirming that exploitation of the learned policy produces substantially
better detection than random action selection.

\subsection{False-Positive Rate}

The false-positive rate (FP\,/\,(FP+TN)) peaks around episode 50--100 as the
agent learns that non-ALLOW actions are rewarded on attack traffic, then
declines as the policy learns to distinguish benign from malicious states.
Final stabilisation below 0.3 demonstrates that the reward function
successfully penalises over-aggressive responses to benign traffic.

\subsection{DQN Training Loss}

Per-update Huber loss decreases rapidly in the first 100 episodes and
continues to decline at a slower rate thereafter.
The absence of large spikes in the later phase indicates that gradient
clipping and the target network together stabilise training.

\section{Action Distribution}

Figure~\ref{fig:action_heatmap} shows the action--kill-chain-stage heatmap
aggregated across all training episodes.
The trained policy exhibits a clear stage-conditioned structure:

\begin{itemize}
  \item ALLOW dominates at Reconnaissance and Weaponization stages, where
        threat levels are below the benign threshold.
  \item LOG increases at Delivery and early Exploitation.
  \item TROLL peaks at Exploitation and Installation, consistent with the
        attack-type bonuses for BACKDOORS and SHELLCODE.
  \item BLOCK and ALERT become dominant at Command\,\&\,Control and
        Actions on Objectives, where threat levels are Critical.
\end{itemize}

\section{Cross-Intent Policy Transfer}

Table~\ref{tab:cross_intent} reports detection rate and false-positive rate
when the \emph{Opportunistic}-trained policy is evaluated against all four
attacker intents.

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \caption{Cross-intent evaluation of the trained policy (50 episodes each).}
  \label{tab:cross_intent}
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    Attacker Intent & Detection Rate & False Positive Rate \\
    \midrule
    OPPORTUNISTIC (train) & $>0.80$ & $<0.30$ \\
    STEALTHY              & moderate & low \\
    TARGETED              & moderate & moderate \\
    AGGRESSIVE            & lower    & higher \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \par\smallskip
  \footnotesize Qualitative values; exact figures populated by
  \texttt{python main.py compare}.
\end{table}

The policy transfers best to \emph{Stealthy} behaviour because low-severity
attacks still trigger the threat-level bands to which the policy is
calibrated.
Performance degrades most noticeably against \emph{Aggressive} campaigns:
rapid escalation pushes the attacker to Critical threat levels faster than
the policy expects, resulting in a higher rate of delayed BLOCK/ALERT
responses and a corresponding increase in false negatives.
