\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

\section{Interpretation of Training Dynamics}

The two-phase training curve --- high-variance exploration followed by
rapid reward improvement --- is consistent with the theoretical properties
of $\varepsilon$-greedy DQN.
The replay buffer requires a warm-up period before the distribution of
sampled transitions is sufficiently diverse for stable gradient updates.
The correlation between the end of the high-$\varepsilon$ phase (around
episode 100) and the onset of detection-rate improvement confirms that the
network has learned a meaningful $Q$-value surface before exploitation begins.

The simultaneous early rise and late decline of the false-positive rate
reflects the agent working through a reward-shaping trade-off: initially
it learns that non-ALLOW actions yield positive rewards on attack traffic,
then it learns to discriminate the state features that indicate benign
traffic (low threat level, low escalation rate, NORMAL attack type) and
returns the correct ALLOW action in those cases.

\section{Effectiveness of the Reward Function}

The reward function design encodes three layers of domain knowledge:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Proportional responses} --- the base matrix steers the
        agent toward graduated responses (LOG for low threats, TROLL/BLOCK
        for medium, ALERT/BLOCK for critical) rather than a binary
        detect/ignore policy.
  \item \textbf{Kill-chain urgency} --- the $1.5\times$ late-stage penalty
        on negative rewards amplifies the cost of missing an attack at
        Installation, Command\,\&\,Control, or Actions on Objectives,
        encouraging the agent to be more aggressive as campaigns progress.
  \item \textbf{Honeypot-specific intelligence} --- the bonus for TROLL on
        persistent attack types (BACKDOORS, SHELLCODE, WORMS) incentivises
        the tarpitting strategy that simultaneously gathers intelligence
        and wastes attacker resources.
\end{enumerate}

The action heatmap in Figure~\ref{fig:action_heatmap} shows that all three
layers are active in the learned policy, suggesting the reward signal is
informative and the DQN is capable of learning the multi-modal response
structure.

\section{Generalisability Across Attacker Intents}

The degradation of the trained policy against the \emph{Aggressive} intent
highlights a structural limitation: the Markov transition matrices for
different intents produce qualitatively different threat-level time series.
\emph{Aggressive} campaigns escalate to Critical threat levels in fewer
steps, creating a temporal mismatch with the policy's calibrated response
timing.
Two approaches could address this:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Multi-intent training} --- exposing the agent to a mixture
        of intents during training would broaden the state distribution and
        encourage a more robust policy.
  \item \textbf{Intent inference} --- augmenting the state vector with an
        online intent posterior (estimated from the recent attack sequence)
        would allow the policy to condition its responses on inferred
        attacker behaviour.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Limitations}

\paragraph{Synthetic data only.}
Feature distributions are parametric approximations inspired by UNSW-NB15
rather than fits to the actual dataset.
Real network traffic contains long-range temporal correlations, protocol
interactions, and noise sources that are not captured by the per-step
independence assumption.

\paragraph{Defender action does not affect attacker trajectory.}
In the current formulation the attacker's Markov chain evolves independently
of the defender's actions.
In practice, a BLOCK or ALERT response may deter or redirect an attacker.
Modelling the attacker as a best-responding agent (e.g.\ using multi-agent
RL or game-theoretic methods) would produce a more realistic adversarial
dynamic.

\paragraph{Classifier is not integrated into the DQN state.}
The Random Forest classifier provides attack-type predictions that are
logged but not fed into the DQN observation vector.
Replacing ground-truth labels with classifier outputs would test whether
the policy is robust to classification noise --- a necessary step before
real-world deployment.

\paragraph{Single-episode evaluation.}
The cross-intent results report means over 50 episodes, but variance across
episodes is not reported.
A more rigorous evaluation would include confidence intervals and
statistical tests comparing intent-conditional performance distributions.

\section{Design Choices and Trade-offs}

\paragraph{LayerNorm over BatchNorm.}
LayerNorm normalises across features rather than across the batch dimension,
making it independent of batch size.
For a DQN trained on mini-batches of 64 samples drawn from a diverse replay
buffer, LayerNorm avoids the instability that arises when BatchNorm
statistics are computed from a heterogeneous mini-batch.

\paragraph{Hard target network update.}
A hard copy every 150 steps (rather than a Polyak soft update) was chosen
for simplicity.
Soft updates with a small mixing coefficient ($\tau \approx 0.005$) could
provide smoother target stabilisation, but the current setting proved
adequate for convergence.

\paragraph{Synthetic training data for the classifier.}
Generating classifier training data from the parametric feature distributions
avoids the need for a labelled real-world dataset and enables rapid
re-training with arbitrary class balance.
The risk is that the classifier is over-fit to synthetic data and may not
generalise to real traffic --- a known challenge in intrusion detection
research.
