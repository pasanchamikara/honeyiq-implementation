\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Modern networked systems face an ever-escalating threat landscape in which
attackers progress through structured sequences of exploitation stages.
Static rule-based defences cannot adapt to the dynamic, intent-driven nature
of contemporary adversaries.
This thesis presents \textbf{HoneyIQ}, a simulation framework in which a
Deep Q-Network (DQN) agent learns to operate a honeypot against a
Markov-chain-driven attacker modelled on the Lockheed~Martin Cyber Kill
Chain~\citep{hutchins2011intelligence}.

The attacker subsystem generates realistic network traffic by sampling
parametric feature distributions derived from the UNSW-NB15 intrusion
detection dataset~\citep{moustafa2015unsw}.
Four attacker intent profiles --- \emph{Stealthy}, \emph{Aggressive},
\emph{Targeted}, and \emph{Opportunistic} --- are encoded as intent-specific
Markov transition modifiers, producing qualitatively distinct attack
campaigns.

The defender combines a Random Forest classifier~\citep{breiman2001random}
for attack-type identification with a DQN~\citep{mnih2015humanlevel} that
selects one of five honeypot responses (ALLOW, LOG, TROLL, BLOCK, ALERT)
from a 24-dimensional state vector.
A composite threat-level signal integrates attack severity, kill chain
progress, escalation rate, and cumulative attack pressure.

Experimental results demonstrate that a policy trained on the
\emph{Opportunistic} intent achieves a mean detection rate above 0.8 while
maintaining a false-positive rate below 0.3, and transfers with moderate
degradation to the \emph{Stealthy} and \emph{Targeted} profiles.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} deep reinforcement learning, Deep Q-Network,
honeypot, cyber kill chain, intrusion detection, Markov chain, cybersecurity.
